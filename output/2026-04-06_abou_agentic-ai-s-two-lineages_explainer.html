Research Explainer · Abou Ali, Dornaika & Charafeddine (2026)

Agentic AI has two fundamentally different lineages, but most researchers keep pretending it's one

A PRISMA-based review of 90 studies reveals that symbolic and neural AI agents work on incompatible principles, and treating them as a single tradition creates confusion in architecture, governance, and deployment alike.

What this survey set out to fix

The Agentic AI field has a terminology problem. Researchers routinely describe modern LLM-based agent systems (AutoGen, CrewAI, LangChain) using concepts borrowed from 1980s symbolic AI: Belief-Desire-Intention models, perceive-plan-act-reflect loops, explicit goal stacks. Abou Ali and colleagues call this conceptual retrofitting, and they argue it is actively harmful. An LLM agent does not maintain a symbolic knowledge base or execute a deterministic plan. It generates stochastic next-tokens in a prompt pipeline. Pretending otherwise obscures how these systems actually work, and it misleads people who need to evaluate, govern, or deploy them.

To cut through the confusion, the authors propose a dual-paradigm taxonomy that sorts every agentic system into one of two lineages. The symbolic/classical lineage descends from rule-based expert systems, MDPs, POMDPs, and cognitive architectures like BDI and SOAR. It relies on algorithmic planning, persistent symbolic state, and deterministic (or probabilistic) logic. The neural/generative lineage descends from deep learning and the transformer revolution. It relies on stochastic generation, prompt-driven orchestration, and context-window management. Both aim at autonomous, goal-directed behaviour. They get there by entirely different mechanisms.

What the review found

The authors ran a PRISMA-compliant systematic search across IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, arXiv, SpringerLink, ScienceDirect, and Google Scholar, starting from 165 initial records and distilling them down to 78 core studies plus 12 foundational symbolic papers for context. Two coders independently classified every paper by paradigm, achieving a Cohen's Kappa of 0.82 (substantial agreement). Three patterns jumped out of the data.

First, paradigm-market fit: high-stakes, regulated sectors like healthcare and legal tech overwhelmingly favour symbolic or heavily constrained neural architectures, because they need auditability and determinism. Finance and education, which prize adaptability and work with messy, unstructured data, run almost entirely on neural orchestration frameworks. Second, a governance gap: the ethics and safety literature is almost exclusively focused on neural systems (prompt injection, hallucination, alignment). Governance research for modern symbolic systems barely exists, which is worrying given that symbolic AI still runs safety-critical infrastructure. Third, a temporal paradigm shift: symbolic and hybrid architectures dominated research from 2018 to 2021; after 2022, neural orchestration frameworks took over completely.

Why the distinction matters for governance

The paper's sharpest practical contribution is its demonstration that AI governance cannot be architecturally agnostic. When a symbolic system fails, the failure comes from flawed logic or an unhandled edge case, and you can trace it back to a specific rule a programmer wrote. Liability is messy but tractable. When a neural system fails, the failure emerges from stochastic outputs, prompt injection, or biases baked into training data. Liability is diffuse and, as the authors put it, there is a profound "attribution gap" that existing legal frameworks are not prepared for.

Transparency follows the same split. A symbolic agent's reasoning is inherently explainable: you can print the rule-firing trace. A neural agent's "reasoning" is an emergent property of model activations, and post-hoc explanations (SHAP, LIME) are approximations at best. Demanding "full explainability" from a neural agent is not just hard, it may be technologically impossible with current methods. The authors argue this means regulation needs paradigm-specific compliance mechanisms, not a single checklist applied to everything labelled "AI agent."

Hybrid systems compound the problem. A neuro-symbolic architecture inherits the governance challenges of both paradigms simultaneously: you need to audit the deterministic logic and monitor the neural components for stochastic failures. The oversight burden roughly doubles.

The road ahead is hybrid

The survey's central strategic claim is that the future of Agentic AI is neither symbolic nor neural, but deliberately hybrid. Neural components would handle perception, pattern recognition, and operation on unstructured data. Symbolic modules would enforce reasoning constraints, safety checks, and auditability. The authors envision future multi-agent ecosystems composed of specialised agents: some highly neural for creative tasks, some highly symbolic for regulatory compliance, communicating through standardised interoperability protocols.

They identify nine specific research gaps, from the lack of paradigm-specific benchmarks (symbolic systems need tests for logical soundness; neural systems need tests for hallucination under pressure and prompt-injection resilience) to the absence of reliable external memory architectures for neural agents, whose context-window limitations produce "severe amnesia across sessions." The paper also flags infrastructure dependence as a structural risk: neural systems require vast cloud compute, centralising power and creating supply-chain vulnerabilities that symbolic systems, with their lighter computational footprint, largely avoid.
