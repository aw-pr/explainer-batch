Research Explainer · Vishwakarma, Agarwal et al. (2025)

AI agents promised to automate enterprise work, but they can't finish more than four tasks in ten

EnterpriseBench is the first benchmark that simulates real corporate environments with fragmented data, access controls, and cross-department workflows. The best LLM agents top out at 41.8% task completion, while humans acting as agents hit 70%.

41.8% peak task completion by the best LLM agent (o1-mini with gold planning on DSPy), measured across 500 enterprise tasks spanning HR, IT, Sales, SWE, and Business Operations

500 enterprise tasks across five domains, each requiring an average of 3 tool calls to complete, covering search, CRUD, and unanswerable categories

70% accuracy achieved by human agents performing the same tasks, at the cost of 10× longer completion time (8.5 minutes vs. 50 seconds per task)

What They Built

EnterpriseBench is a simulated corporate sandbox. It contains 1,260 synthetic employees distributed across HR, IT, Sales, Software Engineering, and Business Operations departments, complete with email threads (4,500), chat logs (3,000), GitHub repos (1,000), CRM records (30,195), IT tickets (163), and 24 policy documents. All of this data is interconnected through an entity-relationship model curated by domain experts, and protected by a role-based access control system with six permission levels.

The benchmark itself consists of 500 tasks. Roughly 65% are search tasks (finding information across fragmented sources), 30% are CRUD tasks (creating, reading, updating, or deleting records), and 5% are deliberately unanswerable queries where the agent should recognise it lacks permission. Each task requires an average of three tool calls, meaning the agent must plan a multi-step workflow, select the right applications, and chain results together.

The team also built an automated task generation pipeline: pick a domain, sample an employee persona, retrieve that persona's contextual data, decompose a goal template into subtasks, and iteratively validate the output against a quality checklist. Human experts rated 80% of generated tasks as realistic and suitable for the benchmark. That pipeline makes EnterpriseBench scalable without needing access to actual corporate data.

What They Found

Five models were tested under four planning strategies (no planning, Chain-of-Thought, ReAct, and gold planning) across two agent frameworks (LangChain and DSPy). The best overall result came from o1-mini using gold planning on DSPy, scoring 0.62 on the Prometheus-2 rubric. With ReAct (the best strategy that doesn't cheat by handing the agent the correct plan), o1-mini managed 0.38 on DSPy and 0.35 on LangChain. GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet clustered around 0.28 to 0.34 under ReAct. Open-source Llama models were significantly weaker: Llama-3.1-8B peaked at 0.16.

Gold planning (giving the agent the exact correct plan in advance) improved scores by 40–50% over ReAct. That gap is the headline finding. These agents don't fail because they lack reasoning power or tool-use ability in isolation. They fail because they can't figure out which tools to call, in which order, with which parameters. The planning is the bottleneck.

Human agents, given the same tools, achieved 70% accuracy, but took an average of 8 minutes and 30 seconds per task compared to 50 seconds for the LLM agent. Precision and speed sit on opposite ends of a seesaw, and current LLM agents are firmly planted on the fast-but-wrong side.

A fine-tuned Qwen3-8B model trained on just 1,200 samples reached 0.29 with SFT+DPO, matching GPT-4o's Chain-of-Thought performance. That's a small model on minimal data nearly matching the best proprietary model on a particular strategy, which suggests the benchmark's data generation pipeline produces useful training signal.

Where Agents Break Down

An error analysis on 100 tasks with the o1-mini ReAct agent revealed a taxonomy of failure modes. The largest single category was task decomposition errors (20 of 69 failures), where the agent misunderstands the structure of the task and produces an incorrect plan. Next came wrong tool or app selection (18 failures), followed by partial factual coverage (14 failures), where the agent gets the right answer but omits critical structured details like employee IDs or policy names.

Hallucination accounted for 8 failures. The agent fabricated policy names, dates, or entities rather than relying on retrieved context. Final-step execution errors (7 failures) showed agents could get every intermediate step right and still botch the final synthesis. Context retrieval problems were the smallest category at just 2 failures, suggesting that the retrieval infrastructure works, but the agent doesn't know what to do with what it retrieves.

Why It Matters

Enterprise AI vendors routinely demonstrate agents that can, say, look up a customer record or draft an email. EnterpriseBench tests the realistic version of that sales pitch: can your agent create a GitHub repo, look up the manager's email from the employee database, draft a notification, and send it, all while checking that the requesting employee actually has permission to do each of those things? The answer, for now, is "sometimes, poorly."

The 40–50% improvement from gold planning over ReAct tells us exactly where to focus. Better retrieval models won't help much (retrieval errors were the rarest failure mode). Better planning and environment-grounding will. The authors note that continual learning, step-level validation, and constrained decoding are the most promising directions. The benchmark also demonstrates that small models trained on domain-specific data (Qwen3-8B with SFT+DPO) can match large proprietary models, offering a cheaper path for organisations willing to invest in fine-tuning.

The gap between human accuracy (70%) and agent accuracy (31–41.8%) gives us a ceiling for near-term ambition and a floor for acceptable performance. Enterprises need agents that are at least as good as a slow human before they'll trust them with real workflows. We're about halfway there.
