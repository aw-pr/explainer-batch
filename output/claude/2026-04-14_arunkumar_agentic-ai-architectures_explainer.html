LLM agents now perceive, plan, and act autonomously, but the engineering to make them reliable is just getting started

A comprehensive survey proposes a six-dimension taxonomy for agentic AI, mapping how single-loop reasoning agents are evolving into hierarchical multi-agent systems with standardized tool use, controllable orchestration, and formal evaluation along cost, latency, accuracy, security, and stability.

From text generators to autonomous agents

The paper frames a fundamental shift in how AI systems are built. Generative AI maps inputs to static outputs. Agentic AI does something different: it perceives its environment, reasons about what to do, acts on the world, and then loops back to check what happened. The authors formalize this as a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) control loop, where the agent system is defined by five components: a state space, an observation space, an internal memory space, a tool/action space, and a policy.

At each timestep, the agent receives a partial observation through a perception function (which can include vision encoders like CLIP or text wrappers), updates its mutable memory by combining the new observation with prior reasoning traces and execution feedback, generates a latent reasoning step (the plan), and then selects and executes an action from the tool space. The environment reacts, producing feedback that closes the loop.

This is not just a theoretical nicety. The formalization matters because it shows where concrete engineering decisions sit: how perception is grounded, how memory is structured and pruned, how planning depth trades off against cost, and how actions are sandboxed. The rest of the paper is organized around these decisions.

The six-dimension taxonomy

The paper's central organizing contribution is a taxonomy that decomposes the agentic AI landscape into six connected dimensions. Figure 1 maps these out visually, and the authors use them as the skeleton for the entire survey.

Core Components covers the agent's interface with the world: perception (now multimodal, handling screenshots, audio, and 3D point clouds), memory (from context windows through vector databases to paged systems like MemGPT), action/tools (API calls, code-as-action, computer use, embodied VLA), and profiling (system prompts and dynamic roles). Cognitive Architecture addresses how agents reason, from linear ReAct loops through Tree of Thoughts branching to native inference-time reasoning models like OpenAI's o1/o3 line. Learning spans in-context few-shot methods, agent-specific fine-tuning, and alignment via RLHF/RLAIF. Multi-Agent Systems covers topologies (chain, star, mesh) and interaction patterns (debate, role-playing, SOPs). Environments groups where agents operate: digital (web, OS, enterprise), embodied (robotics, games), and specialized (healthcare, science, finance). Evaluation and Safety applies the CLASSic framework of cost, latency, accuracy, security, and stability.

Planning architectures and the cost of thinking harder

The cognitive architecture section is where the paper gets most practically useful. It traces a clear evolution: ReAct introduced interleaving reasoning with environment interaction, but its linear loop is myopic and prone to error propagation. Tree of Thoughts added explicit branching and backtracking, at the cost of exponential token consumption. LATS connected agent planning to Monte Carlo Tree Search. And now, frontier reasoning models (the o1/o3 line) internalize parts of this search at inference time, trading a controllable compute budget for improved accuracy.

The authors are blunt about the tradeoff. Table 4 in the paper shows that hierarchical architectures like ReAcTree achieve the strongest reasoning depth and modular long-horizon solving, but at medium-high token complexity. LATS and Tree of Thoughts push token consumption to very high levels. Standard Chain-of-Thought remains cheapest but offers no error recovery. Figure 4's radar chart makes this visual: hierarchical agents dominate on reasoning depth and tool proficiency but crater on cost efficiency.

Reflection mechanisms provide a partial answer. Reflexion stores natural-language critiques of failures and conditions future attempts on those lessons. Self-Refine implements iterative generate-critic-revise loops. PALADIN trains recovery behaviors on failure trajectories. But none of these come free. The paper frames this as the core engineering tension of agentic AI: intelligence and cost are currently coupled, and decoupling them is the field's most pressing practical problem.

Multi-agent systems and the rise of flow engineering

The paper identifies a major industry shift: production multi-agent systems are moving from open-ended chat loops toward explicit workflow graphs. LangGraph treats agent execution as graph traversal with typed state, checkpoints, and controlled cycles. OpenAI Swarm emphasizes lightweight handoff routines between specialist agents. The developer designs the control structure; the LLM fills in local decisions. The authors call this flow engineering.

Three collaboration topologies dominate. Chain (waterfall) topologies, used by MetaGPT and ChatDev, enforce standard operating procedures with sequential handoffs between roles like Product Manager, Architect, and Engineer. ChatDev reports a 30% reduction in bugs compared to single-agent coding. Star (hub-and-spoke) topologies, used by AutoGen, route tasks through a central controller to specialized workers, supporting human-in-the-loop oversight. Mesh (swarm) topologies enable decentralized interaction for creativity, debate, and social simulation, with systems like Generative Agents and SocioVerse scaling to 10,000+ agents.

The paper also highlights hierarchical verification as a reliability mechanism. MAKER introduces cross-examination, using distinct Verifier agents to challenge Worker outputs, enabling million-step reasoning chains with near-zero error accumulation. The Good Parenting framework proposes Reviewer agents that filter Child agent outputs through safety-focused system prompts, reportedly reducing hallucination rates by up to 100% in controlled environments. The hard problems: hallucination in action, loops, and prompt injection

The paper's most valuable section may be its catalogue of failure modes. When an agent can execute actions (modify files, run code, operate a desktop), hallucinations stop being wrong text and become wrong actions with real consequences. The authors call this hallucination in action. Cascading failures are common in multi-step loops like ReAct: a single bad early step propagates through the entire trajectory.

Infinite loops are equally pernicious. Agents frequently get stuck retrying failed actions without modifying their strategy. WebArena benchmarks report success rates often below 15% on long-horizon tasks, partly because agents cannot recognize when they are caught in a local optimum. The asynchronous planning benchmark Robotouille shows an even starker gap: synchronous agents achieve 47% success, but performance plummets to 11% when tasks involve variable temporal delays.

Security is the third front. Prompt injection turns agents into confused deputies when malicious instructions are embedded in web pages, documents, or tool outputs. The risk is amplified in computer-use settings where agents interpret screenshots and execute mouse/keyboard actions. Prompt-only defenses like PromptArmor are brittle against adaptive attackers. The authors argue that robust security is a systems problem requiring layered mitigations: constrained tool permissions, compartmentalized sandboxes, explicit user confirmation for sensitive actions, and independent audit components. The Model Context Protocol (MCP) provides a standardized connector layer with governance boundaries, but the attack surface continues to expand.

BOTTOM LINE

The central design question for AI systems is shifting from how to prompt a model to how to program and control a complete agent system. Progress will not come from model scale alone. It requires architectures that integrate perception, memory, tools, and collaboration in ways that are not only powerful, but controllable, auditable, and aligned with the constraints of real-world deployment. The paper's six-dimension taxonomy provides a practical map for navigating that engineering challenge.
