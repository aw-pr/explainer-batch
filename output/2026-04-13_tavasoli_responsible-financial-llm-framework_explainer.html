Research Explainer · Tavasoli (2025)

Banks want LLMs for everything, but the paper says ask six hard questions first

A new six-decision framework guides financial institutions from 'do we even need an LLM?' through data governance, risk, ethics, ROI, and deployment, treating responsible adoption as a sequential checklist rather than an afterthought.

Published April 2025

KEY CONTRIBUTION

The paper introduces a structured six-decision framework for deploying Large Language Models in financial services. It forces institutions to answer whether an LLM is genuinely needed before addressing data governance, risk management, ethical oversight, ROI justification, and implementation strategy, arguing that most existing guidance skips the early feasibility question entirely.

The gap the framework fills

Existing literature on LLMs in finance tends to split into two camps. One group surveys model architectures and benchmarks, covering things like FinBERT, BloombergGPT, and FinGPT. The other focuses on responsible-AI principles, fairness metrics, or explainability tools. Tavasoli, Sharbaf, and Madani argue that neither camp asks the most basic question first: does your use case actually require an LLM, or would a rule-based NLP pipeline do the job more cheaply and more transparently?

By front-loading that feasibility check, the framework connects early-stage decisions (like how much model complexity you need and what data you can legally feed it) to downstream choices about monitoring, retraining, and vendor contracts. The authors explicitly target multiple audiences: executives aligning AI with business strategy, compliance teams wrestling with GDPR and Basel III, data scientists balancing performance against interpretability, and ethics committees worried about bias in credit scoring.

Six decisions, in order

The framework walks an institution through a strict sequence. Decision 1 asks whether an LLM is even warranted. If a task involves structured data extraction or routine compliance checks, a simpler, more auditable system probably wins. Decision 2 locks down data governance: classification of data sensitivity, anonymization, encryption, and data-localization rules that determine whether you can use a public cloud at all. Decision 3 layers on risk management, covering explainability tools like SHAP and LIME, human-in-the-loop oversight for high-stakes outputs, and adversarial stress-testing in sandbox environments.

Decision 4 addresses ethics beyond what compliance requires, including bias audits across demographic groups, workforce transition planning, and preserving customer autonomy so that AI recommendations stay advisory rather than authoritative. Decision 5 demands a rigorous cost-benefit analysis that accounts for tangible metrics (process automation rate, cost per transaction) and intangible ones (Net Promoter Score, reputational index). Only after all five gates are cleared does Decision 6 arrive: choosing open-source vs. proprietary, in-house vs. vendor, and the right adaptation method, whether that is prompt engineering, parameter-efficient fine-tuning, full fine-tuning, or retrieval-augmented generation.

The authors present a two-by-two implementation matrix mapping model openness against deployment strategy, with real-world examples in each quadrant. ANZ Bank shifted from OpenAI's API to fine-tuning an open-source LLaMA variant for cost and regulatory reasons. Morgan Stanley went the opposite direction, partnering with OpenAI to integrate GPT-4 into wealth management. Neither choice is wrong; the point is that it should come last, after the governance groundwork is done.

The iterative loop that makes it practical

The framework is not a one-pass checklist. A unified decision flowchart shows that pilot-test failures feed back to earlier decisions for remediation. If a pilot reveals insufficient ROI, you revisit Decision 5. If it surfaces privacy conflicts, you return to Decision 2. Once a model reaches production, continuous monitoring tracks five metric categories: performance (accuracy, recall, domain coverage), trust and safety (factual accuracy, fairness indices), operational efficiency (latency, throughput, scaling costs), user experience (satisfaction, task completion rates), and risk management (security monitoring, anomaly detection).

Major issues, like a new data-localization mandate or systemic bias discovery, trigger a return to Decision 2 for a full data-governance reassessment. Minor problems, like small latency spikes, get handled through incremental prompt refinements or lightweight model updates. The paper provides detailed tables of operational safeguards (data classification, federated learning, anonymization, explainability tooling, incident response) and maps each safeguard to the decision it supports. This makes the framework less of a theoretical exercise and more of a living operational document.

What the framework does not do

This is a conceptual framework, not an empirical study. There are no controlled experiments, no measured accuracy improvements, and no before-and-after deployment data from a specific institution. The case examples (ANZ Bank, Morgan Stanley) are illustrative references rather than original findings. The paper also does not quantify the cost of implementing its own recommendations, something that would strengthen the ROI argument considerably.

That said, the contribution is genuinely useful. Most financial institutions adopting LLMs today skip straight to Decision 6, picking a model and a vendor before they have answered any of the harder questions about data governance, bias, or whether an LLM is needed at all. Forcing the conversation through five prior gates, each with concrete sub-questions and failure modes, is a corrective that the industry needs. The real test will be whether practitioners treat it as a living governance tool or file it alongside every other compliance checklist they already ignore.
