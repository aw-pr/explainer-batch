Research Explainer · Staufer (2026)

AI agents are shipping fast, but their safety documentation is barely there

A systematic index of 30 deployed AI agents finds that most developers publish capability benchmarks while disclosing almost nothing about safety evaluations, ecosystem interactions, or societal impact.

Published February 2026

55% of all safety-related annotation fields (133 out of 240) had no publicly available information across 30 indexed agents

77% of agents (23/30) had no documented third-party safety testing, audits, or red-teaming results

70% of agents (21/30) do not disclose their AI nature to end users or third parties by default

What the Index actually measures

The 2025 AI Agent Index is a structured database covering 30 deployed agentic AI systems, each annotated across 45 fields grouped into six categories: product overview, company and accountability, technical capabilities, autonomy and control, ecosystem interaction, and safety. The researchers annotated only publicly available information, using documentation, websites, demos, published papers, and governance documents. They also contacted every developer and gave them four weeks to correct the draft annotations. Only 4 out of 30 responded with substantive comments.

To qualify for the Index, an agent had to pass through three filters. It needed to be genuinely agentic (autonomous, goal-complex, environment-interacting, and general-purpose), impactful (high search volume, billion-dollar developer, or membership in a major safety commitment), and practical (publicly available, deployable without engineering expertise, and general-purpose). From 95 candidates, 30 made the cut. The result is not a ranking but a transparency audit: a picture of what developers choose to tell the world about systems that act on their users' behalf.

The safety disclosure gap

The headline finding is stark. Out of 1,350 total annotation fields, 198 had no publicly available information at all. Safety and ecosystem interaction accounted for the vast majority of those gaps: 133 missing safety fields and 45 missing ecosystem fields. That means for the average agent, more than half of the safety-related questions the researchers asked could not be answered from any public source.

Only four agents in the entire Index have agent-specific system cards: ChatGPT Agent, OpenAI Codex, Claude Code, and Gemini 2.5 Computer Use. The rest rely on documentation for the underlying foundation model, which tells you about the model's conversational safety but nothing about how the agent plans, selects tools, manages memory, or interacts with external services. 25 out of 30 agents disclose no internal safety evaluation results whatsoever. Third-party testing is documented for just 3 out of 30.

The researchers call this a weaker form of safety washing, a pattern where companies publish high-level safety frameworks and ethics commitments but selectively withhold the empirical evidence that would let outsiders actually assess risk. Nine agents publish capability benchmark results while having little or no safety disclosure, suggesting that developers are willing to show you what their agents can do but not what might go wrong.

How agents interact with the web (and why nobody knows)

Browser-based agents like Perplexity Comet operate at the highest autonomy levels (L4-L5) in the Index, meaning once you prompt them, you cannot easily intervene until they finish. They browse the web, fill out forms, and make purchases. They also, in many cases, pretend to be human. Six out of 30 agents explicitly use Chrome-like user-agent strings and residential IP addresses, mimicking normal browser traffic. Only 7 out of 30 publish stable user-agent strings and IP ranges that website operators could use to identify them.

The tension between agents and existing web norms is acute. Only 6 agents explicitly state that their crawlers respect robots.txt. Perplexity argues its agents "work just like a human assistant" and should therefore be exempt from scraping restrictions. Cloudflare documented undeclared crawlers from Perplexity using generic Chrome signatures to evade blocking. Amazon threatened legal action over Comet not identifying itself as an agent. ChatGPT Agent is the only system in the Index that uses cryptographic request signing, which would let a website verify that a request genuinely came from OpenAI.

For 16 out of 30 agents, there is no public statement at all about robots.txt compliance, CAPTCHA handling, or web access methods. This is not a niche governance question. As more tasks get delegated to agents that operate autonomously on the open web, the absence of identification standards and consent mechanisms becomes a structural problem for the entire internet.

Foundation model concentration and the accountability gap

Nearly every agent in the Index runs on one of three model families: GPT, Claude, or Gemini. Only frontier labs themselves (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) and Chinese developers operate their own proprietary models. Everyone else is a customer. Enterprise agents tend to be model-agnostic, with 9 out of 30 supporting user selection across providers, but consumer-facing agents are typically locked to a single provider's API.

This creates a fragmented accountability structure. A deployed agent is the product of a foundation model, an orchestration layer, tool integrations, user-designed configurations, and a deployment context. No single entity controls the full stack. The researchers illustrate this with three case studies: OpenAI controls both the model and the scaffolding for ChatGPT Agent. HubSpot controls the orchestration for Breeze but delegates agent design to its customers and has limited visibility into downstream deployment. Perplexity's Comet sits in the browser and has direct access to the deployment environment but limited control over the models it calls.

The practical consequence is that model-level safety evaluations, even good ones, cannot capture risks that emerge from tool access, memory systems, planning depth, and deployment context. Regulators and enterprise buyers who rely on model-only documentation may be getting false assurance. The researchers argue that evaluations need to target tools in use, not just conversational safety, and that risk information needs to flow across ecosystem layers rather than stopping at the model provider's API boundary.

KEY FINDING

The AI agent ecosystem is deploying fast and documenting slowly. Over half of safety-related fields for 30 major agents have no public information. Browser agents operate at the highest autonomy levels while having the largest documentation gaps. The structural pattern is clear: companies will tell you what their agents can do, but not what guardrails are in place, how risks were evaluated, or how the agent identifies itself on the open web. Until that changes, policymakers and enterprise buyers are operating on incomplete information about systems that are already making consequential decisions.
