Research Explainer · Bravyi (2024)

LDPC quantum codes cut memory overhead, but move the hard problem into hardware connectivity

Bravyi and colleagues show that bivariate bicycle quantum LDPC codes can preserve logical qubits with far fewer physical qubits than surface-code patches in simulation. The catch is not subtle: the codes need degree-6 connectivity, two planar coupler layers, and long-range links that current superconducting layouts do not yet make easy.

The core move

The paper builds quantum low-density parity-check codes from bivariate bicycle constructions, written as QC(A,B), where the check matrices come from commuting bivariate shift matrices. Each code has length n = 2lm, weight-6 checks, and each data qubit participates in six checks.

That sounds abstract, but the engineering point is clear. These are non-local quantum codes with a constant-depth syndrome circuit: n data qubits, n ancillary check qubits, and seven CNOT layers per syndrome cycle, independent of code length. The authors are trying to keep the qubit count low without letting the measurement schedule grow into treacle.

The near-term comparison is about qubits, not just thresholds

The paper compares the [[144,12,12]] BB code with surface-code patches for storing 12 logical qubits at roughly pL = 2 × 10^-7 from p = 10^-3. The surface-code value is stated as more than 3,000 physical qubits, so 3,000 is shown as a conservative lower bound.

Why the layout is plausible

The main architectural trick is that the Tanner graphs have thickness at most 2. In plainer terms, the degree-6 connectivity can be decomposed into two edge-disjoint planar degree-3 subgraphs.

That matters for superconducting qubits because a completely arbitrary non-local code is easy to admire and hard to build. A two-layer planar coupling structure is still demanding, but it gives hardware designers a shape to aim at rather than a mathematical shrug.

What the simulations say

The authors numerically search finite-size BB codes and evaluate five examples: [[72,12,6]], [[90,8,10]], [[108,8,10]], [[144,12,12]], and [[288,12,18]]. Their pseudo-thresholds range from 0.0048 to 0.0069, close to the surface-code threshold under the same broad circuit-level framing.

At physical error rate p = 10^-3, the [[144,12,12]] code reaches pL = 2 × 10^-7 per syndrome cycle using 288 total physical qubits. The larger [[288,12,18]] code uses 576 physical qubits and reaches pL = 2 × 10^-12. That is the headline: useful-looking memory performance at a qubit count that is no longer science fiction by default.

What remains unproved

This is a numerical paper, not an experimental demonstration. Logical error rates were simulated for independent circuit-level faults, with direct estimates for p >= 10^-3 and lower-error behaviour extrapolated using a fitting formula. Several circuit distances are also listed as upper bounds, and the [[144,12,12]] circuit distance is conjectured to equal 10 rather than proved in the notes provided.

The decoder is heuristic too. The authors adapt BP-OSD by simulating every distinct single fault offline, then solve the online syndrome instance approximately as an optimisation problem. That may be a good practical decoder, but it is not a proof that the reported circuits are being decoded optimally.

The hardware blockers are concrete rather than ceremonial:

  1. Second coupler layerThe construction needs two planar degree-3 coupling layers with low enough loss to preserve the error budget.
  2. Seven connections per qubitEach qubit needs six code-related couplings plus control, which is a crowded design target for superconducting hardware.
  3. Long-range couplersFor the 144-qubit code, some buses may be long enough to require frequency engineering beyond the standard circuit-QED model.

The bigger implication

The paper changes the near-term quantum memory conversation. If the [[144,12,12]] result transfers from simulation to hardware, 12 logical qubits could be stored for nearly one million syndrome cycles with 288 physical qubits at p = 10^-3. A surface-code route to a similar target is reported to need more than 3,000 physical qubits.

The unresolved question is scale. The authors explicitly say it remains unclear whether these BB-code examples can be scaled while keeping a high encoding rate. So the result is not a finished roadmap to large fault-tolerant machines. It is something narrower and more interesting: a serious candidate for making small quantum memories arrive earlier than the surface-code arithmetic suggested.

BOTTOM LINE

Bivariate bicycle LDPC codes offer a striking simulated overhead reduction for quantum memory: surface-code-like thresholds with far fewer physical qubits. The bill comes due in hardware connectivity, decoder assumptions, and unresolved scaling.
