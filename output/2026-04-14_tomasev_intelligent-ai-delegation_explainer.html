Research Explainer · Tomašev (2026)

AI agents need to delegate tasks to each other, but nobody has built the trust infrastructure yet

A new framework from Google DeepMind proposes that intelligent delegation between AI agents and humans requires dynamic trust, verifiable task completion, and adaptive coordination, not just simple task splitting.

Published February 2026

KEY CONTRIBUTION

The paper introduces a comprehensive framework for 'intelligent delegation,' defined as a sequence of decisions involving task allocation that also incorporates transfer of authority, responsibility, accountability, clear role specifications, and mechanisms for establishing trust. The framework is built around five pillars: dynamic assessment, adaptive execution, structural transparency, scalable market coordination, and systemic resilience, each mapped to specific technical protocols. It applies to human-to-AI, AI-to-AI, and AI-to-human delegation in complex, web-scale agentic networks.

Why task splitting is not delegation

Current multi-agent AI systems break problems into pieces and farm them out. That counts as task decomposition, but Tomašev, Franklin, and Osindero argue it falls well short of delegation. Real delegation, the kind that makes human organisations function, bundles task allocation with the transfer of authority, the assignment of responsibility, mechanisms for trust, and accountability for outcomes. Strip those away and you get brittle pipelines that cannot adapt when something goes wrong.

The paper draws on a rich set of organisational analogies. The principal-agent problem, familiar from economics, maps directly onto a world where an AI agent may pursue its own objectives at the expense of the delegator's intent. Span of control, borrowed from management theory, asks how many sub-agents a single orchestrator can meaningfully oversee before errors creep in. Authority gradients, first named in aviation safety research, describe how large capability gaps between delegator and delegatee can suppress legitimate pushback, a dynamic already visible in sycophantic language models that refuse to challenge instructions.

The authors also highlight the zone of indifference: the range of instructions an agent executes without critical scrutiny. In current systems this zone is defined by safety filters and system prompts. As delegation chains grow longer, a broad zone of indifference lets subtle intent mismatches propagate downstream, with each agent acting as an unthinking router rather than a responsible actor. Intelligent delegation, they argue, requires engineering dynamic cognitive friction so agents know when to stop and ask questions.

Five pillars of the framework

The proposed framework rests on five requirements, each mapped to specific technical protocols. Together they cover the full lifecycle of a delegated task, from the moment an agent realises it cannot do the job alone through to verified completion and payment.

The five pillars of intelligent delegation and their corresponding protocol mechanisms:

  1. Dynamic AssessmentContinuous inference of a delegatee's competence, reliability, resource availability, and intent. This feeds into task decomposition and task assignment decisions, moving beyond static reputation scores to real-time state monitoring.
  2. Adaptive ExecutionDelegation decisions should not be static. Delegators retain the ability to switch delegatees mid-execution when performance degrades, resources spike, or external conditions change. The paper details both internal triggers (verification failures, budget overruns) and external triggers (priority changes, security alerts).
  3. Structural TransparencySub-task execution in AI-to-AI delegation is currently too opaque for meaningful oversight. The framework enforces auditability through monitoring protocols and verifiable task completion, so that both successful and failed executions can be attributed.
  4. Scalable Market CoordinationDelegation protocols must work at web scale. The framework proposes decentralised market hubs where delegators advertise tasks and agents bid competitively, supported by trust and reputation systems and multi-objective optimisation of cost, speed, privacy, and quality.
  5. Systemic ResilienceWithout safety protocols, delegation diffuses responsibility until nobody is accountable. The framework operationalises responsibility through permission handling with privilege attenuation, security layers against threats from malicious delegators and delegatees alike, and protections against ecosystem-level risks like Sybil attacks and cognitive monoculture.

How delegation actually works in the framework

The paper's most concrete contribution is a detailed nine-component protocol stack. Task decomposition comes first: a delegator breaks a complex objective into sub-tasks, optimising the execution graph for modularity and verifiability. The framework introduces contract-first decomposition as a binding constraint, meaning no task gets delegated unless its outcome can be precisely verified. If a sub-task is too subjective or complex to verify, the system recursively decomposes it further until the pieces match available verification capabilities like formal proofs or automated unit tests.

Task assignment happens through decentralised market hubs rather than centralised registries, which the authors argue will not scale. Agents submit competitive bids, negotiate terms in natural language, and formalise agreements as smart contracts that pair performance requirements with verification mechanisms and automated penalties. These contracts are bidirectional: they protect the delegatee as rigorously as the delegator, with compensation terms for cancellation and renegotiation clauses for unforeseen events.

Monitoring spans five dimensions: target (outcome vs process), observability (direct vs indirect), transparency (black-box vs white-box), privacy (full transparency vs zero-knowledge proofs), and topology (direct vs transitive across delegation chains). For high-stakes tasks, the authors propose that delegatees publish progress milestones to a blockchain, with algorithmic triggers that fire when performance degrades. For privacy-sensitive work, zero-knowledge proofs let a delegatee prove a computation was performed correctly without revealing the underlying data.

Trust and reputation form the market's backbone. The paper distinguishes reputation (public, verifiable history) from trust (private, context-dependent threshold set by each delegator). Reputation can be implemented via immutable performance ledgers, webs of trust using decentralised identifiers and verifiable credentials, or behavioural metrics that evaluate how a task is performed rather than just the final output. High-trust agents earn more autonomy and lighter monitoring, creating strong economic incentives to build and maintain a good track record.

The ethical stakes of letting agents manage humans

The paper is unusually direct about the risks of AI-to-human delegation. Algorithmic management systems in ride-hailing and logistics already delegate managerial functions to human workers, and a growing body of research links these systems to degraded job quality, stress, and health risks. The authors argue that present-day AI-human delegation fails because it ignores human welfare and long-term social externalities.

They identify several ethical hazards. The erosion of meaningful human control is one: as delegation chains lengthen, human overseers develop their own zone of indifference, rubber-stamping decisions without genuine scrutiny. The paper warns against creating a moral crumple zone where humans retain nominal authority but lack real control, existing in the chain only to absorb liability. The proposed fix is context-aware cognitive friction that lets routine tasks flow freely but forces manual intervention and justification for high-uncertainty or high-stakes decisions.

De-skilling is another concern the paper takes seriously. This is Bainbridge's classic paradox of automation: as AI agents handle the routine work that builds expertise, human operators lose the hands-on experience required to handle the edge cases and failures that still get escalated to them. The authors suggest that intelligent delegation frameworks should occasionally introduce deliberate inefficiencies, routing some tasks to humans specifically to maintain their skills, and developing curriculum-aware task routing systems that track junior team members' skill progression and strategically allocate learning opportunities.
