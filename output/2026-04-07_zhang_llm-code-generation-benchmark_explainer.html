Research Explainer · Zhang & Kothari (2025)

DeepSeek-R1 and GPT-4.1 lead the LLM coding race, but every model still needs a human looking over its shoulder

A 944-problem LeetCode benchmark across five programming languages finds that the best AI code generators solve roughly 89% of problems on the first try, while the worst barely crack 50%. Correctness is only half the story: even top models produce algorithmically sluggish code that would fail real-world time constraints.

What They Did

Zhang and Kothari assembled 944 LeetCode problems covering greedy algorithms, sorting, binary search, and tree-based tasks, then had six large language models generate one-shot solutions in Python, C++, Java, JavaScript, and Go. Each solution was submitted to LeetCode's own test harness, which runs it against every test case (some problems have nearly 10,000) and reports whether it compiled, ran without crashing, returned correct output, and finished within the platform's time limit.

The six models span two categories. General-purpose models include DeepSeek-R1 (671B parameters, reasoning-optimised), DeepSeek-V3 (685B, Mixture-of-Experts), GPT-4.1 (estimated 1.8T), Claude 3.7 Sonnet (estimated 200B), and Llama-3.3-70B. Qwen2.5-Coder-32B is the only code-specialist. All models received identical structured prompts with role specification, problem description, constraints, a code snippet skeleton, sample test cases, and instructions to output executable code only. Temperature was fixed at 0.1 and top-p at 0.95 across the board.

Beyond raw pass rates, the study tracked four failure modes separately: compile-time errors (syntax problems), runtime errors (crashes), functional failures (wrong answers), and algorithmic suboptimality (correct answers that exceeded time limits). A separate 202-problem subset of efficiency-critical tasks tested whether adding a single line ("Optimize the time complexity of your algorithm") to the prompt changed results.

What They Found

DeepSeek-R1 and GPT-4.1 pulled clearly ahead of the pack. Averaged across all five languages, DeepSeek-R1 hit a Pass@1 of roughly 88%, with GPT-4.1 close behind at 85%. Claude 3.7 and DeepSeek-V3 occupied a strong middle tier (around 83–85%), while Qwen2.5-Coder trailed at 72% and Llama-3.3 sat at the bottom with roughly 53%.

The gap widens when you look at where failures happen. Compile-time errors were virtually absent for Python and JavaScript across every model, but Go was a graveyard for syntax correctness: Qwen2.5-Coder and Llama-3.3 both hit about 7% compile failures in Go alone. Runtime errors were generally low (under 2% for most models), with one notable exception: Llama-3.3 crashed 5.4% of the time in C++, likely tripped up by pointer arithmetic and memory management.

The real separator was functional failure, the rate at which code compiled and ran but produced wrong answers. DeepSeek-R1 kept this below 9% across all languages. Llama-3.3 sat above 30% in every language, meaning nearly one in three of its compilable, non-crashing solutions returned incorrect output. That is not a rounding error. That is a model that writes code which looks plausible, executes without incident, and delivers the wrong result.

Algorithmic suboptimality told a similar story. DeepSeek-R1's time-limit-exceeded rate stayed under 2.3% in every language. Llama-3.3 breached 10% in JavaScript and 9.5% in Python, defaulting to brute-force approaches where optimised algorithms were needed. When the researchers added the optimisation hint to the 202-problem subset, DeepSeek-R1 and GPT-4.1 improved markedly (DeepSeek-R1 dropped from 4.95% to 2.97% in Python). Llama-3.3 barely budged. Claude 3.7 showed essentially no change, suggesting it was either already at its ceiling or simply ignored the instruction.

Why It Matters

The headline number (89% Pass@1 for the best model on Python) sounds impressive until you consider what the remaining 11% means at scale. A team generating hundreds of code solutions per day with DeepSeek-R1 would still produce dozens that silently fail, some crashing at runtime, others returning wrong answers without any visible error. And that is the best model. The worst passes barely half its problems on the first try.

The study also punctures the assumption that code-specialist models automatically outperform general-purpose ones. Qwen2.5-Coder, the only dedicated code model in the lineup, landed in the middle of the pack, well behind the much larger general-purpose DeepSeek-R1 and GPT-4.1. Model scale and reasoning architecture mattered more than specialised fine-tuning, at least on this benchmark.

Language choice matters too, though not in the way you might expect. Python and JavaScript were syntactically friendliest (zero compile errors everywhere), but they also produced more algorithmic suboptimality. Statically typed languages like C++ and Java forced fewer time-limit failures, possibly because their stricter structure nudges models toward more explicit, and therefore more efficient, implementations. Go, the youngest language in the set, was the clear weak spot across the board.

One Line Changes Everything (for Some Models)

The prompt engineering experiment is the most practically useful part of the study. Adding a single sentence asking the model to optimise time complexity cut DeepSeek-R1's suboptimality rate nearly in half on the 202-problem subset. GPT-4.1 and DeepSeek-V3 responded almost as well. This was not a sophisticated chain-of-thought intervention or multi-turn conversation. It was seven words.

Llama-3.3, by contrast, barely responded. Its suboptimality rates dropped from around 40% to around 37%, a marginal improvement that suggests the model either lacks the algorithmic knowledge to optimise or cannot translate a high-level instruction into a concrete implementation change. Claude 3.7's non-response was different in character: its rates were already moderate and stayed flat, implying the model had already reached its performance ceiling without the hint. The practical lesson: prompt engineering works, but only on models with enough latent capability to act on the instruction.
