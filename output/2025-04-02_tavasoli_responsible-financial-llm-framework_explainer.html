Research Explainer · Tavasoli (2025)

Banks rushing to adopt LLMs get a six-step framework, but it starts by asking if they need one at all

A new decision-centric blueprint walks financial institutions through feasibility, data governance, risk, ethics, ROI, and deployment choices before committing to large language models.

Published April 2025

KEY CONTRIBUTION

The authors propose a structured six-decision framework specifically for financial institutions considering LLM adoption. Rather than jumping straight to model selection, the framework forces organizations to first ask whether an LLM is even warranted, then systematically work through data governance, risk management, ethical oversight, ROI justification, and only then choose between open-source versus proprietary, in-house versus vendor-hosted deployment paths. The framework connects early-stage decisions to ongoing monitoring through iterative feedback loops, so choices about data sensitivity or model complexity directly shape how the system is governed after launch.

The first question most banks skip

Most frameworks for deploying AI in finance start with model selection. This one starts with a more uncomfortable question: do you actually need an LLM? The authors argue that many routine financial tasks, from structured data extraction to standard compliance checks, work perfectly well with traditional NLP pipelines or rule-based systems. LLMs shine when you need deep contextual reasoning over unstructured text, like parsing nuanced investor communications or interpreting ambiguous regulatory language. For everything else, they introduce opacity, cost, and operational overhead that simpler tools avoid entirely.

The paper provides a decision flowchart for this first gate. It asks whether the task involves unstructured, language-intensive content requiring deep contextual understanding. If not, you default to simpler approaches. If so, you then evaluate whether strict accuracy and consistency requirements can be met, whether the interpretability demands of regulators can be satisfied, and whether the computational costs are justified by the incremental gains. Only when all these hurdles are cleared does an LLM or hybrid model become a viable option. This is a notably sceptical starting position for a paper about LLM adoption.

Data governance and risk before you pick a model

Decisions two and three cover data governance and risk management, and the paper treats them as prerequisites that must be resolved before any deployment choice is made. On the data side, the framework requires rigorous classification of information as public, confidential, or mission-critical. It then demands institutions confirm the legality of letting an LLM process each category, enforcing safeguards like anonymization, encryption, and secure enclaves. The authors highlight a particularly sharp concern: LLMs can unintentionally memorize and potentially disclose sensitive training data, making data localization rules and cross-border transfer restrictions a genuine architectural constraint, not just a compliance checkbox.

For risk management, the paper acknowledges that traditional Model Risk Management (MRM) practices struggle with LLMs because third-party pretrained models provide limited visibility into their internal workings. The recommended approach is adaptive governance: continuous monitoring and iterative validation post-deployment rather than one-off pre-launch checks. The authors push for adversarial prompt testing, simulations of market volatility, and human-in-the-loop oversight for sensitive tasks. They also provide a detailed governance table covering six dimensions, from interpretability and validation to bias audits and cybersecurity, each with specific failure modes if neglected.

Ethics and ROI come before implementation, not after

The framework's fourth decision embeds ethical oversight as an explicit gate rather than an afterthought. The authors argue that LLMs trained on historical financial data inherently risk replicating past inequities in credit scoring, investment recommendations, and customer segmentation. They recommend pre-deployment bias testing across demographic factors, adoption of established frameworks like Singapore's FEAT principles (Fairness, Ethics, Accountability, Transparency), and the creation of dedicated ethical committees that regularly reassess AI deployments against evolving societal expectations. Workforce impact gets equal billing: institutions should treat LLM adoption as augmentation rather than replacement, with clear upskilling pathways and transparent communication about changing roles.

Decision five forces a rigorous cost-benefit analysis before committing to implementation. The paper distinguishes between tangible returns like process automation rates and cost-per-transaction reductions, and intangible benefits like brand perception and innovation positioning. The authors propose a KPI matrix spanning economic metrics, user-centric measures (both tangible and intangible), and strategic indicators like time-to-market and AI adoption index. A phased adoption pathway with initial pilot projects and targeted KPIs helps institutions validate ROI predictions against real-world outcomes before scaling up.

The implementation matrix and the case for hybrid everything

Only after passing the first five gates does the framework address model and deployment selection. The authors map the decision space along two axes: model openness (open-source vs. proprietary) and deployment strategy (in-house vs. vendor-hosted), creating a four-quadrant implementation matrix. Each quadrant carries distinct trade-offs. A large bank fine-tuning an open-source LLaMA variant in-house gets full autonomy and data control at the cost of heavy engineering investment. A fintech using OpenAI's API for chatbots gets speed to market and minimal development load, but accepts limited model insight and high vendor reliance.

The paper treats adaptation methods (prompt engineering, parameter-efficient fine-tuning, full fine-tuning, and Retrieval-Augmented Generation) as a separate but interlinked choice. Their comparison table is one of the more useful artifacts in the paper, laying out each method's cost profile, hallucination risk, explainability, and best-fit scenarios. RAG emerges as particularly well-suited to finance because each answer can be tied back to a specific source document, simplifying auditing. The authors' consistent recommendation is hybrid configurations: pair deterministic rules for compliance-critical decisions with LLM-driven language insights, blend multiple adaptation methods, and expect the chosen approach to evolve as the institution's AI maturity grows.
