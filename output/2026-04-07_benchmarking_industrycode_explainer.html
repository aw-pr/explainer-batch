Research Explainer · Zeng et al. (2026)

The best AI can write Python, but ask it for MATLAB and it starts making things up

IndustryCode, the first multi-domain, multi-language benchmark for industrial code generation, finds that even top models solve fewer than half of real-world engineering problems and collapse on specialist languages like Stata and MATLAB.

What They Built

IndustryCode is a benchmark of 579 sub-problems drawn from 125 main industrial challenges, sourced from the actual production codebases of working engineers. The domains span finance, automation, aerospace, remote sensing, chemical engineering, and more. The languages cover Python, C++, MATLAB, and Stata, reflecting the polyglot reality of industrial software rather than the Python-dominated world of existing benchmarks like HumanEval and SWE-bench.

Each main problem is hierarchically decomposed into modular sub-problems, mimicking the way engineers naturally break a system into functions. To solve a main problem, a model must implement each sub-module and then integrate them into a working whole. The benchmark was hardened against data contamination through manual reformulation and adversarial revision, with domain experts verifying that all 579 tasks produce results within engineering tolerance of ground-truth values.

What They Found

Twenty-one models were evaluated, ranging from proprietary flagships (Claude 4.5 Opus, GPT-5.2, Gemini-3-pro) to open-weights systems (Qwen3-Max, DeepSeek-V3.2-Exp). Claude 4.5 Opus came first on both sub-problems (68.1%) and main problems (42.5%), followed by Gemini-3-pro and Claude 4.5 Sonnet. The gap between sub-problem and main-problem scores is striking: even the best model drops by 25.6 percentage points when it must wire components together into a complete system. Models can handle individual functions; they struggle with integration.

Performance also varies wildly by language. Python and C++ sub-problems clustered in the 45–72% range for strong models, while Stata accuracy hovered near 33–50% for most, and several models scored zero on Stata main problems entirely. MATLAB sat in between, with accuracy often below 50% on main problems. The authors attribute this to a simple data-availability gap: Python and C++ have billions of training tokens on GitHub, while Stata and MATLAB codebases are mostly proprietary and locked behind academic paywalls.

Domain performance told its own story. Finance and IT sub-problems saw average accuracy above 59%, benefiting from their algorithmic certainty. Semiconductors and Microelectronics sat at the bottom at 33.5%, because models trained on sequential software languages keep misapplying patterns to concurrent hardware-description logic. Qwen3-Max beat all proprietary models in IT (86.5%) and Machinery Manufacturing (83.3%), showing that open models optimised through Mixture-of-Experts routing can dominate specific sectors even if they trail on aggregate scores.

Why Models Fail

The error analysis is more revealing than the leaderboard. Syntax errors accounted for 32.8% of all failures, and problem misinterpretation for another 30.2%. Reasoning failure, the error mode you might expect to dominate industrial tasks, was only 8.4%. Models do not struggle to think through the logic; they struggle to speak the right language and read the specification correctly. Hallucination (19.6%) rounds out the picture: when domain knowledge is absent, models invent non-existent APIs rather than admitting ignorance.

Turning on explicit "thinking mode" (chain-of-thought reasoning) produced a paradox. Reasoning failures dropped from 11.8% to 0.8%, confirming that the chain-of-thought mechanism genuinely helps with logical decomposition. But context confusion exploded from 1.9% to 29.2%, and problem misinterpretation doubled to 30.4%. The longer reasoning trace consumes token budget, introduces semantic leakage into the code output, and over-engineers solutions with unnecessary complexity. The authors call this the "cost of reasoning," a Pareto trade-off where improved logic comes at the expense of instruction adherence and syntactic stability.

Why It Matters

Existing code benchmarks (HumanEval, MBPP, SWE-bench) test models on the kind of code most commonly found on the internet: Python, web applications, algorithmic puzzles. IndustryCode tests them on the kind of code that runs factories, prices derivatives, and simulates spacecraft trajectories. The gap between benchmark scores and real-world utility has been an open question; this paper quantifies it. A model that scores 80%+ on HumanEval can score under 50% on IndustryCode's sub-problems and under 25% on its main problems.

The implication is practical. Companies evaluating LLMs for industrial deployment should not trust general-purpose benchmarks as proxies. MATLAB fluency, cross-module integration, and domain-specific precision are distinct capabilities, and current models have not saturated any of them. The 25-point gap between sub-problem and main-problem accuracy suggests that the bottleneck is not function-level generation but system-level orchestration, the ability to keep state consistent across a long engineering workflow. That is the frontier.
