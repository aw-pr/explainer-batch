Research Explainer · Wang (2025)

Letting AI models think longer helps, but the gains hit a wall you can predict

A new probabilistic model shows that both parallel sampling and sequential rethinking follow the same saturation curve, and derives the exact budget beyond which extra computation is wasted.

Published June 2025

+23.3pp accuracy gain on AIME 2024 for a 1.5B model using parallel scaling at N=32 vs. vanilla inference

r = 0.803 Pearson correlation between predicted and observed saturation points for 7B model with parallel scaling

83.6% accuracy of a 1.5B model with parallel scaling on MATH-500, surpassing the 7B model's unscaled 82.0%

The premise: more thinking, more accuracy, forever?

Large reasoning models like DeepSeek-R1 and OpenAI's o1 already spend extra computation internally, generating long chain-of-thought traces before answering. A natural next step is to pile on even more inference compute: sample dozens of candidate answers in parallel, or ask the model to rethink its answer across multiple rounds. This strategy is called test-time scaling, and it works. A 1.5-billion-parameter model that normally scores 30% on competition-level maths problems can hit 53.3% just by generating 32 parallel answers and taking a majority vote.

But the researchers at Hong Kong Polytechnic University noticed something practitioners often feel intuitively: those gains taper off fast. The jump from 1 to 4 generations is dramatic. From 16 to 32, you burn twice the tokens for a sliver of improvement. The paper asks a precise question: can you calculate in advance exactly when to stop spending?

Two flavours of scaling, one saturation curve

The paper examines two fundamental strategies. Parallel scaling generates N independent candidate answers and picks the best one (typically via majority vote). Sequential scaling asks the model to rethink its previous answer round by round, treating the process as a Markov chain with an absorbing "correct" state. The two approaches look very different operationally, but the authors show they converge to the same mathematical form. In both cases, the probability of solving a problem after N attempts is 1 minus (1 minus p) to the power N, where p is the per-attempt success probability.

This means the marginal gain from each additional attempt decays exponentially. The paper defines the scaling plateau as the point where that marginal gain drops below a chosen threshold epsilon. From this, they derive a closed-form saturation point N* that depends on just three quantities: the model's maximum achievable performance, the per-attempt success probability, and your tolerance for diminishing returns. The formula is identical for parallel and sequential scaling, which the authors call a "unified mathematical structure" underlying both paradigms.

The predictions hold up, especially for parallel sampling

To test whether the formula actually works, the researchers ran DeepSeek-R1-Distill models (1.5B and 7B parameters) across AIME 2024, AIME 2025, MATH-500, and GPQA, varying N from 1 to 32. They estimated per-problem success probabilities from a validation split, plugged them into the saturation formula, and compared predictions against the empirically observed plateau on a held-out test set.

The results are strongest for parallel scaling. The Pearson correlation between predicted and observed saturation points reaches 0.803 for the 7B model and 0.768 for the 1.5B model. Sequential scaling correlations are lower (0.575 for the 7B model), which makes sense given that the independence assumption is a rougher fit when each round depends on the previous one. Still, even the weaker correlations are statistically significant at p < 0.001.

One particularly striking finding: the tiny 1.5B model with 32 parallel samples scores 83.6% on MATH-500, beating the 7B model's unscaled 82.0%. Test-time scaling can substitute for model size, up to the plateau.

What this means for practitioners (and what it doesn't)

The practical takeaway is straightforward. If you can estimate a model's per-problem success probability from a small validation run, you can plug it into the saturation formula and get a per-problem budget that avoids wasting tokens. The authors frame this as "scaling over scaling," adding test-time compute on top of models that already do internal chain-of-thought reasoning. Their model tells you where the second layer of scaling stops paying for itself.

The caveats are honest and worth noting. The model assumes independence between attempts, which is a decent approximation for parallel sampling but weaker for sequential rethinking where errors can propagate. The parameters (maximum performance, success probability, threshold) are task-specific and model-specific, so you need a calibration step for each new setting. And the entire analysis applies to verifier-free approaches; adding an external reward model or verifier could change the shape of the curve entirely.

Despite those limits, the core insight is clean and useful: the maths of diminishing returns is the same whether you sample in parallel or iterate in sequence. Both hit the same wall, for the same reason, at a point you can calculate before you spend the compute.

KEY TAKEAWAY

Both parallel sampling and sequential rethinking follow an identical exponential saturation curve. A simple closed-form formula, requiring only a per-problem success probability and a tolerance threshold, predicts when extra test-time compute stops being worthwhile. For parallel scaling, predictions correlate with reality at r = 0.80, strong enough to guide real inference budgets.
