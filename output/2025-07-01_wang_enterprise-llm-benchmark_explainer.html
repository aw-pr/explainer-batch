Research Explainer · Wang (2025)

Atlassian built an enterprise LLM benchmark, and every model failed at company knowledge

A 14-task, 9,700-sample evaluation framework grounded in Bloom's Taxonomy reveals that even the best LLMs score below 0.20 on proprietary enterprise knowledge tasks, while open-source reasoning models like DeepSeek R1 match or beat GPT-4o on most other enterprise workloads.

Published 2025

0.20 maximum correctness score any model achieved on enterprise acronym memorization (out of 1.0), exposing a fundamental gap in proprietary knowledge

8–5 open-source models beat GPT-4o on eight tasks, tied on one, and lost on only five across the 14-task benchmark

9,700 curated evaluation samples spanning six cognitive levels from Remember to Create, built with a scalable LLM-assisted annotation pipeline

Why standard benchmarks miss what enterprises actually need

If you have tried picking an LLM for internal tooling, you have probably leaned on MMLU scores, Chatbot Arena rankings, or Big-Bench results. The Atlassian team argues those numbers are close to useless for enterprise work. Public benchmarks are increasingly saturated, so the gap between a frontier model and last quarter's release barely registers. More importantly, none of them test what matters inside a company: retrieving facts from internal wikis, converting natural language to Jira queries, or detecting hallucinations about your own products.

The paper proposes a 14-task benchmark built from Atlassian's own data sources: Confluence pages, Rovo chat logs, customer feedback, Slack queries, and developer documentation. To structure the tasks, the authors use Bloom's Taxonomy, a six-level cognitive framework originally designed for education. The levels range from Remember (can the model recall an internal acronym?) through Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, and Create (can it generate coherent new content?). Each task gets a two-digit identifier: the first digit marks the cognitive level, the second marks the task within that level.

How they built 9,700 labeled samples without breaking the budget

Labeling nearly ten thousand enterprise-specific samples by hand would be prohibitively expensive. Instead, the team built a five-stage pipeline. Raw data gets collected from internal sources, cleaned to remove noise and duplicates, then annotated by GPT-4o acting as a labeler. For tasks requiring enterprise or web knowledge (like software engineering and ML engineering questions), they add Corrective Retrieval-Augmented Generation (CRAG), which retrieves relevant documents, grades their relevance, and falls back to web search when retrieval quality is low. After labeling, an LLM-as-a-Judge pass (using the G-Eval framework) scores label quality. Human experts then review only the low-confidence subset, correcting errors without having to touch every sample.

Two tasks still required fully manual labels: NL2JQL conversion (translating natural language to Jira Query Language) and the LLM-as-a-Judge task itself, where ground-truth search query scores came from human annotators. Two others, summarization and content generation, are open-ended and have no single correct answer, so they rely on model-based evaluation metrics like relevance and coherence rather than ground-truth labels. The final benchmark covers roughly 600 samples per task, a number the authors validated through an ablation study showing evaluation stability at that size.

The results: open-source models are competitive, but nobody knows your company

The headline finding is blunt: every model is terrible at enterprise-specific knowledge. On acronym memorization, the best correctness score was 0.20 (Llama 3.3 70B). On factual question-answering from Confluence data, DeepSeek R1 led with just 0.18 out of 1.0. These are not difficult questions by academic standards. They are the kind of thing a new employee could answer after reading an internal wiki page. The models simply have never seen this information.

Where models did well, they did well across the board. Summarization relevance scores ranged from 0.81 to 0.88, and content generation coherence reached 0.84 to 0.97. Toxicity and bias metrics were near zero for all large models, which makes sense given the enterprise data was collected under strict ethical guidelines. The more interesting gaps appeared in mid-level cognitive tasks. GPT-4o dominated sentiment analysis (97.2% accuracy versus 90% for DeepSeek R1) and named entity recognition (77.4% exact match versus 70.5% for Llama 4 Scout). But open-source models won on eight of fourteen tasks overall, tied on one, and lost on just five.

The DeepSeek R1 distilled into Llama 3.3 70B provided a useful natural experiment. Compared to the original Llama 3.3 70B, the distilled reasoning variant won on 11 tasks, tied on one, and lost on only two. Sentiment analysis jumped from 72.1% to 84.3%. That said, reasoning models stumbled on judgment tasks: on the LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation, GPT-4o achieved a Spearman correlation of 0.47 while DeepSeek R1 managed only 0.38. The authors attribute this to overthinking, where the extended chain-of-thought reasoning actually hurts on tasks that require quick, calibrated scoring rather than deep analysis.

What this means for picking and fine-tuning enterprise models

The practical takeaway is that no model ships ready for enterprise work. The benchmark makes a clear case for continuous pre-training on proprietary data: the Remember-level tasks prove that even 671 billion parameters cannot compensate for never having seen your internal documentation. If you are choosing a base model for fine-tuning, the open-source options are now genuinely competitive. DeepSeek R1 matched or beat GPT-4o on most tasks and led significantly on content generation (coherence of 0.97 versus 0.91).

The annotation pipeline may be as valuable as the benchmark itself. Most companies face the same bottleneck: mountains of unlabeled internal data and no budget for large-scale human annotation. The combination of LLM-as-a-Labeler, CRAG for knowledge grounding, LLM-as-a-Judge for quality filtering, and targeted human review of low-confidence samples offers a replicable template. The authors position this explicitly as a blueprint, and for once the claim seems justified. The approach took Atlassian from raw Confluence pages to a 9,700-sample labeled dataset without an army of annotators.

The one finding that should give pause is the hallucination result. Even the best-performing model (DeepSeek R1) hallucinated on 75.8% of Atlassian-specific questions, and Llama 3.3 70B hit 91.1%. These are not edge cases. They are routine questions about the company's own software products. Until models are grounded in proprietary knowledge through RAG or continued pre-training, deploying them for enterprise knowledge tasks without safeguards is asking for trouble.

KEY FINDING

Open-source LLMs now match proprietary models on most enterprise tasks, but every model tested fails catastrophically on company-specific knowledge. The highest correctness score on proprietary factual recall was 0.18 out of 1.0, and hallucination rates on enterprise questions exceeded 75% across the board. Fine-tuning on internal data is not optional, it is the entire point.
