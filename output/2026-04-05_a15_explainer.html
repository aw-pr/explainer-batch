Research Explainer · Survey paper

Agentic AI expands the attack surface, and the obvious defences do not hold up for long

This paper is a literature survey rather than a new experiment, but the evidence it assembles is hard to wave away. Once you give models tools, memory, and autonomy, old LLM jailbreak problems turn into practical security failures, and the survey shows that point with numbers from prompt injection, autonomous exploitation, and evaluation studies.

What this survey actually does

This is not a lab study with one model, one dataset, and a carefully stage-managed result. It is a literature survey. The authors read across prior work and sort agentic AI security into a taxonomy of threats, defences, benchmarks, and unresolved problems. That sounds tidy, but the interesting part is that the taxonomy follows how agents really behave: they call tools, they keep state, they delegate to other agents, and they act in environments that can deceive them.

The survey groups threats into five broad buckets: prompt injection and jailbreaks, autonomous cyber-exploitation and tool abuse, multi-agent and protocol-level threats, interface and environment risks, and governance or autonomy concerns. It applies the same organising instinct to defences. Some controls focus on the agent itself, some on the user, some on the surrounding system, and the paper also separates training-based interventions from training-free ones such as policy enforcement, sandboxing, confinement, and monitoring. That structure matters because it stops the discussion drifting into the usual mush of “just add guardrails”.

What the evidence says

The headline result is not subtle. The survey reports that Lupinacci and colleagues found 94.4% of state-of-the-art LLM agents vulnerable to prompt injection, 83.3% vulnerable to retrieval-based backdoors, and 100% vulnerable to inter-agent trust exploits. If you were hoping that agent systems had quietly solved the old prompt injection mess, the numbers suggest the opposite.

The paper also points to attacks that adapt to the defence rather than politely failing when they meet one. Adaptive indirect prompt injection reportedly penetrated eight different defences with a 50% success rate. CrossInject, a cross-modal attack strategy, improved effectiveness by at least 30.1% across tasks. Those are not edge-case failures caused by one careless deployment. They show attackers exploiting the fact that agents now ingest instructions through retrieved text, web content, files, images, and other systems that developers do not fully control.

Then there is the practical offensive capability. In one cited study, GPT-4-based agents achieved an 87% success rate on one-day vulnerability exploitation tasks when given CVE descriptions, beating tools such as OWASP ZAP and Metasploit. The survey also notes that GPT-4 agents autonomously breached sandboxed websites in website-hacking setups, although performance fell sharply without document access or detailed prompting. The autonomy is real, but it still depends heavily on context and scaffolding. That is not comforting. It just tells you where the blast radius sits.

Why simple metrics fail

The survey is particularly good on evaluation, which is where many security papers become suspiciously convenient. The authors argue that end-state success rates are too blunt for agentic systems. A model that looks safe on average can still occasionally perform a destructive action, and in security that one bad run is the one people remember. So they push for process-aware evaluation: repeated trials, pass@k style distributions, trajectory-level monitoring, human agreement checks, and stability tests across random seeds and task paraphrases.

The examples show why this matters. In WebArena, removing the explicit hint that a task was unachievable improved GPT-4 task success by 14.41%, yet the model's true positive detection of impossible tasks was only 44.44%, and it mislabeled 54.9% of feasible tasks as impossible. An agent can therefore look prudent while actually giving up on tasks it could complete. That is not wisdom. It is confusion wearing a tie.

The survey makes the same point about judges. LLM-as-a-judge setups can swing with prompt wording, trace format, and model choice, and the judges themselves can have security weaknesses. If your evaluator changes its mind when you rephrase the rubric or alter the trajectory formatting, you do not have a stable benchmark. You have a weather report.

Where the defences break

The paper does not claim that defences are useless. It claims, more credibly, that they all come with trade-offs and known failure modes. Training-based prompt injection defences can reduce general capability, and one evaluation found that they did not provide significant protection against adaptive attacks anyway. Prompt augmentation is cheap and easy to bolt on, but attackers can route around it, so it fails as a standalone answer.

User-focused controls improve safety by keeping a human in the loop, but they also cut automation and scalability, and they collapse entirely when the user is the attacker. System-focused controls such as sandboxing and isolation help, but they are not magical either. The survey cites failures including missing file isolation constraints that enabled cross-session leakage and dependency-installation pipeline flaws that opened the door to large-scale code execution attacks. In multi-agent settings, confinement can also reduce the collaboration that made the system attractive in the first place.

Monitoring and detection have the same problem as most static security layers: adaptive adversaries learn the shape of the fence. The survey's practical conclusion is therefore a layered one. You need hybrid protections, runtime monitoring, confinement, and evaluation that treats rare catastrophic behaviour as first-class evidence rather than statistical noise. Neat single-fix stories do not survive contact with agentic systems.
